# How to Price a Painting Job So You Actually Make Money

Most painters price jobs one of two ways: gut feel, or looking at what the competition charges and going slightly lower. Both are slow ways to go broke.

Here's the truth about pricing: your competitors' numbers have nothing to do with your business. Their overhead is different. Their crew costs are different. Their margins are different. If you price against them instead of against your own costs, you're not running a business — you're gambling.

Let's fix that.

---

## The Four Buckets Every Price Must Cover

Every job you quote needs to account for four things. If your price doesn't cover all four, you're either breaking even or losing money — even on jobs that feel profitable.

**1. Materials**
This one's obvious, but get precise. Don't estimate paint — calculate it. Know your coverage rates by surface type. Factor in primer, caulk, masking, drop cloths, and rollers. Most painters undercount materials by 10-15% because they forget the small stuff. If your material budget for a job is $600, build in $660-$690 and you'll almost always land right.

**2. Direct Labor**
What does it cost to get the work done? Take your painter's hourly rate (say, $22/hr), multiply by hours on-site, then add 20-25% for payroll taxes, workers' comp, and any benefits. A painter who costs you $22/hr actually costs $26-$28 fully loaded. Run your estimates with the loaded rate, not the paycheck rate.

**3. Overhead**
This is the number most painters forget entirely, and it's the silent killer. Your overhead includes: truck payments, insurance, tools, software, advertising, your own salary when you're not on the job, and any admin costs. Add it all up annually and divide by your total revenue to get your overhead percentage.

For most small painting companies doing $400K-$800K, overhead runs 15-25% of revenue. That needs to be baked into every quote — not hoped for at the end of the year.

**4. Profit**
Not your salary. Actual business profit. The money you're reinvesting, saving, or distributing after all expenses. If you're not targeting at least 15-20% net profit margin, you're running a charity. Target 20%. Settle for 15%. Never accept less.

---

## The Formula

Here's the simple version:

**Job Price = (Materials + Direct Labor) ÷ (1 - Overhead % - Profit %)**

Example: Materials are $500. Direct labor is $800. Overhead is 20%. Profit target is 18%.

> ($500 + $800) ÷ (1 - 0.20 - 0.18) = $1,300 ÷ 0.62 = **$2,097**

That's your floor. Quote below that and you're giving someone a discount out of your own pocket.

Run this math on your last 10 jobs. I promise at least three of them were underpriced.

---

## The Mistake That Kills Margins

Pricing against competitors is the #1 margin killer in this industry.

You don't know their cost structure. A guy running solo with no insurance, a beat-up van, and no overhead can profitably charge $1,800 on a job that costs you $2,100 to break even. If you chase his number, you lose money and train the customer to expect low prices.

Stop competing on price. Compete on certainty, speed, and quality. Position your company as the option for people who don't want to deal with flakes, missed calls, and sloppy work. Those customers exist in every market. Go get them.

---

## Add-Ons That Increase Your Average Ticket

Once you have the pricing right, the next move is increasing what the average job is worth.

**Cabinet painting:** A kitchen cabinet repaint is $1,500-$3,500 on top of an exterior job. Most homeowners don't think to ask for it. You do.

**Deck staining:** If you're already on the property for exterior paint, offer the deck. It adds $500-$1,500 and costs maybe 4-6 additional hours.

**Garage floors:** Epoxy coating is exploding. Takes one day, materials run $200-$400, charge $900-$1,500. Very high margin work if you learn the process.

**Color consultation upgrade:** Charge $150-$200 for a color consult add-on. Takes 20 minutes. Most customers take it.

The point isn't to upsell aggressively. The point is to let customers buy more from you if they want to — and most will if you ask.

---

## The Rule That Protects You

When in doubt: **never price below your calculated floor.** Not for a referral. Not for a "great neighborhood to market in." Not to beat a competitor.

If a job doesn't pencil at your real costs plus margin, pass on it. Low-margin jobs eat your time, wear out your crew, and leave you too busy to find the good jobs.

Saying no to bad work is one of the highest-leverage moves in this business. It sounds backwards until you do it — and then you wonder why you ever took those jobs in the first place.

Know your numbers. Price for profit. The rest follows.
