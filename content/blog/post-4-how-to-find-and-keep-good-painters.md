# How to Find and Keep Good Painters (Without Paying More Than Anyone Else)

Every painting contractor thinks they have a hiring problem. Most of them actually have a marketing problem.

If your approach to hiring is "post on Indeed and hope someone decent applies," you're going to get the same pool of candidates every other desperate contractor is fighting over. You'll get guys who left their last job because of drama, guys who interview well and disappear after day two, and guys who are technically available because nobody else wanted them.

Good painters are out there. They're just already employed — by someone else. Your job is to make working for you more attractive than where they are now. And here's the thing: that's almost never about the hourly rate.

---

## Treat Hiring Like Marketing

The best operators I know never stop recruiting — even when they're fully staffed. They're always running a low-level intake process so that when they need someone, they have a warm bench.

Here's what that looks like practically:

**Run a Facebook ad in your area** — not a job posting, an ad. Target men aged 21-45 in your metro, copy that says something like: "We're a growing painting company in [city] looking for reliable painters who want consistent work and a crew worth showing up for." A $10/day budget will generate leads. Most companies aren't doing this. That's your advantage.

**Post in local Facebook groups** — every market has a local trades or labor group. Post weekly. Keep it short: "Hiring experienced painters in [city]. Steady work, paid weekly, good crew. DM me." You'll get replies.

**Craigslist still works.** Laugh if you want, but trades workers still use it. A well-written Craigslist posting in most markets will get 10-20 replies in 48 hours. Most of them will be useless. Some won't be.

**Referrals from your current crew are gold.** Tell your best guys: "Send me someone as good as you, and if they last 90 days, you get $300." That's cheap recruiting. People refer people they know can do the job — because their own reputation is on the line.

---

## Screen Fast, Decide Fast

The #1 hiring mistake after failing to recruit is taking too long to decide.

Good painters have options. If you ask them to come in for a "formal interview" with a multi-day turnaround, they'll take a job with the guy who texted them back in an hour. Speed matters.

My process: DM or call back within a few hours. Short 10-minute phone screen. If they pass, bring them in for a paid trial shift (half day, $100-$150 cash). If they show up, work hard, and get along with the crew — offer the job by end of day.

The trial shift is important. You learn more in 4 hours on a job than in 3 phone interviews. And it filters out no-shows fast.

---

## The Onboarding That Actually Keeps People

Most painters quit in the first 30 days — not because the money's bad, but because they felt like they didn't know what was expected, the crew was cliquey, or the work was disorganized and stressful.

Fix this with a real onboarding:

**Day 1:** Walk them through how you do things. Not just painting — communication, how to handle customer questions, where supplies are, how to log hours. Give them a written one-pager. It says: we're organized, we've done this before, and you're not walking into chaos.

**Week 1:** Pair them with your best crew leader. Not a random guy — your best one. First impressions of culture come from whoever they work next to.

**30-day check-in:** A short conversation. "How's it going? Anything confusing? What do you need?" Most employers never do this. Doing it signals that you give a damn, which is rarer than it should be.

---

## What Actually Keeps Good Painters Around

Pay matters — but it's table stakes, not the differentiator.

What actually drives retention:

**Consistency.** Good painters hate uncertainty. If they're not sure how many hours they'll get week to week, they'll look for something steadier. Keep your schedule full and communicate it in advance. Knowing they have 40 hours lined up next week matters more than an extra $1/hr.

**Respect.** Sounds soft. It isn't. Painters who feel like cogs in a machine leave. Ones who feel like valued crew members stay for years. Learn their names, remember small stuff, don't talk down to them in front of customers. This costs you nothing.

**A path forward.** Your best painters want to grow. Give them a lead painter track — more responsibility, more pay, more ownership of a job. Not everyone will take it, but the ones who do become your most loyal employees. They're invested now. They have something to lose by leaving.

**Predictable pay.** Pay weekly. Always on time. Never make a painter wait for his check. This sounds obvious. It isn't — I've heard from guys who left good-paying companies over chronic payroll delays. Your payroll is your word.

---

## The Compounding Effect

When you have a stable core crew — guys who've been with you for a year or more — everything else in your business gets easier. Job quality improves. Customer satisfaction goes up. Your own stress goes down. You spend less time recruiting and more time growing.

The guys who struggle to hire aren't offering less money. They're offering less certainty, less organization, and less respect. Fix those three things, and you'll never have a hiring problem again.

Good painters don't want the highest wage. They want to work for someone who isn't a mess. Be that person, and they'll find you.
