# The Door Hanger Strategy That Generated $2.5M in One Year

Everyone wants to talk about Google ads and Facebook campaigns. And sure — digital works. But I'm going to tell you about the channel that drove more revenue in our business than anything else for several years running, and it has nothing to do with an algorithm:

Door hangers.

I know. You probably tried them once, got a 0.3% response rate, and moved on. That means you did it wrong. When it's done right — right targeting, right timing, right volume, right offer — door-to-door direct marketing for a local painting company is one of the highest-ROI activities in the business. We attributed $2.5M in booked revenue to this channel in a single year. Here's how.

---

## Why Direct Mail Still Crushes for Local Services

Think about the customer journey for a painting job. A homeowner doesn't wake up on a Tuesday and decide they need their house painted that afternoon. It's a considered purchase. They think about it for weeks — maybe months — before they call anyone.

What that means: the homeowner who gets your door hanger in May might call you in July. They're not impulsive buyers. They're gathering information, making a decision, and when the timing is right, they go to whatever they can remember or find.

Your door hanger is sitting on their counter. Your name is the one they know. That's not luck. That's positioning.

Digital ads disappear when the session ends. A physical piece of marketing sits in a kitchen drawer, on a fridge, on a counter. It has a lifespan of weeks or months. The perceived cost in the customer's mind is low — it's not intrusive like an ad. It's information.

---

## Targeting: Where You Drop Matters More Than How Many

This is where most companies screw it up. They buy a bulk drop of 10,000 hangers across random zip codes and wonder why nothing came back.

Targeting for painting is simple once you know the principles:

**Target neighborhoods where jobs are already happening.** When you're working on a street, hang 50-100 hangers on every house within a three-block radius. These homeowners can see your work from the street. They know the neighborhood. They're already primed. Response rates from job-site proximity drops are 3-5x higher than cold neighborhood drops.

**Target by home age and value.** Houses built between 1970 and 2000 need exterior paint on a more consistent cycle than newer construction. You can pull neighborhood demographic data from your county or use services like Melissa Data or USPS EDDM to filter by home value range. Target homes in the $250K-$600K range — high enough to afford quality work, not so high that you're fighting commercial painters for contracts.

**Seasonal timing.** In cold-weather markets, drop in March-April (homeowners are thinking about spring projects), again in August (second push before fall). In warm markets, the window is wider — but avoid the summer slowdown months when budgets tighten. Hit early spring and early fall.

---

## Volume: The Number That Actually Matters

A lot of companies treat door hangers like a campaign. They do a drop, wait for results, and evaluate. That's the wrong model.

Think of it like farming. You drop consistently, season after season, in the same neighborhoods. You build name recognition. By the third or fourth time a homeowner sees your hanger, you're not a random contractor — you're "that painting company that keeps being in this neighborhood." That familiarity converts.

Volume benchmarks:
- **Under $500K revenue:** 1,000-2,000 hangers per week during season
- **$500K-$1M:** 3,000-5,000 per week
- **$1M+:** 5,000-10,000 per week with dedicated distribution staff or a service

At $60-$80 per thousand hangers printed and distributed, you're spending $300-$800/week at scale. If you close 1 job per 500 hangers — a realistic number in a warm neighborhood with a good offer — you're looking at 6-20 additional jobs per week from this channel alone.

## Design: What Makes One Convert

Your door hanger should be designed around one job: making the homeowner want to call before they've read 10 words.

**The headline does the work.** "Free Estimate + 15% Off Spring Exterior Projects — This Neighborhood Only, Offer Expires May 31." That's a reason to call. Not "Quality Painting at Affordable Prices" — that's noise.

**Before/after photos are essential.** People buy visual transformations. Use your best before/after on the front panel. If you don't have strong ones, start photographing every job immediately — this is your most valuable marketing asset.

**Social proof fast.** "4.9 stars on Google · 300+ reviews · Licensed & Insured" in small text near the top. Don't make people wonder if you're legitimate.

**One call to action.** One phone number. One URL if you use one. Make it big. Make it easy to act on.

**Size matters.** A 4.25" × 11" or 3.5" × 8.5" door hanger is standard. It should feel like a real marketing piece — not a flimsy flyer. Heavier card stock (100lb+) communicates quality before they've read a word.

## Tracking ROI on Direct Mail

You can't manage what you don't measure. Set up a dedicated tracking number for your door hangers — not your main business line. Services like CallRail cost $30-$50/month and give you call recordings, lead tracking, and attribution data.

When a lead calls, your team asks: "How did you hear about us?" Log it. Over 90 days, you'll know exactly what your cost per lead and cost per acquisition is from this channel. In our case, we were generating booked jobs at a cost of $180-$250 per closed customer. On a $4,500 average ticket, that's an 18-25x return on ad spend.

When the ROI is that clear, scaling becomes obvious. More hangers, more jobs, more revenue. It's not magic — it's direct response math.

---

## The Offer Formula That Works

Don't distribute door hangers without an offer. A piece that just says "call us for a free estimate" is wasted paper. Every successful direct mail piece has an offer with these three components:

1. **A discount or incentive** — 10-15% off, free color consultation, free deck staining with an exterior booking
2. **A specific deadline** — "Offer expires [date 3-4 weeks out]." Without urgency, people mean to call and never do.
3. **Neighborhood specificity** — "This offer is for [neighborhood name] residents only." It makes them feel selected, not mass-marketed to.

Put these together, back it with good targeting and real volume, and your door hangers will do more for your business than most of what you're spending money on digitally.

The companies ignoring direct mail because it feels old are handing you market share. Take it.
