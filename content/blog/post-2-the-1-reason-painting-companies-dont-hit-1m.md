# The #1 Reason Painting Companies Don't Hit $1M (It's Not What You Think)

Most painting companies never break $1M. They bounce between $300K and $600K for years, the owner works himself into the ground, and eventually he either burns out or decides this is just what the ceiling looks like.

It's not the competition. It's not the market. It's not the economy or material costs or finding good help — though every owner will tell you it's one of those things.

The real answer is simpler and harder to hear: **the owner is the bottleneck.**

---

## You're a Great Painter. That's the Problem.

Most guys who start painting companies are technically excellent. They know how to cut a clean line, how to prep a surface, how to make a house look like a magazine photo. Customers love them. They get referrals. They start getting busy.

And then they hire one or two guys to help, and they stay on the job to make sure it's done right, and they run estimates at night, and they answer every call personally because they don't trust anyone else to represent them well — and before long, they've built a job for themselves that pays like a business but costs them their sanity.

This is the technician trap. Michael Gerber wrote about it in *The E-Myth* 30 years ago and it's still killing painting companies today.

You are not a painting company. You are a painter who owns tools. There's a difference.

---

## The Shift: Working ON It, Not IN It

This isn't a mindset thing. It's a practical restructuring of how you spend your hours.

Right now, if you're under $600K, I'd bet your week looks something like this: Monday through Friday on the job, evenings on estimates and calls, weekends on admin. Maybe 55-65 hours total. You're producing — but you're not building.

Here's the question that breaks the pattern: *What would have to be true for this business to run without you for one week?*

If the answer is "it wouldn't," you've identified your problem. You're the system. And systems don't scale — only *documented* systems scale.

---

## Three First Moves That Change Everything

**First move: Your first real hire.**

Not a painter. Not a laborer. A person who can take something off your plate entirely and own it. For most guys, this is a lead painter who can run a job without you on-site. You pay them $25-$30/hr, stop putting your hands on that job, and free yourself up to sell more work.

You'll resist this because you'll worry about quality. The solution is a documented quality checklist that your lead painter uses on every job — not your memory, not your presence.

**Second move: Your first system.**

Pick the thing that eats the most of your time and build a repeatable process for it. For most painters, it's estimates. Build a template. Use a square footage formula. Know your labor and material costs per unit. Stop pricing from your gut.

When you can hand your pricing formula to someone else and trust them to produce a number within 10% of what you'd quote, you've built a system. That system is worth real money.

**Third move: A real sales process.**

"I run estimates and follow up if they don't call me back" is not a sales process. That's hoping.

A sales process means: every lead gets a follow-up within 24 hours. Every estimate gets a call at 48 hours and 5 days if you haven't heard back. You have a script. You have an objection answer for price. You track your close rate by channel so you know where your best customers come from.

When I built this out, my close rate went from about 35% to 52%. On $800K in quoted work, that's the difference between $280K and $416K in revenue. Same leads. Different process.

---

## The Math of Getting Out of Your Own Way

Let's say you're currently doing $600K, working 60-hour weeks, taking home $80K.

You hire a lead painter for $65K/year. You lose 10% of the detailed work you'd normally catch personally. But now you have 20 hours a week to sell.

With those 20 hours, you run 8-10 additional estimates per week. At a 40% close rate and a $4,500 average ticket, that's 3-4 additional jobs per week. At 48 working weeks, that's $650K-$865K in additional revenue per year.

Even if your margins compress slightly from the delegation, you're looking at $1.2M-$1.5M in year one of getting out of the way. Your take-home doubles. You work fewer hours.

The bottleneck was always you. Removing yourself from the field doesn't cost you the business — it *is* the business.

---

## Start Monday

Don't wait until you feel ready. You won't. The week you're reading this, write down every task you did in the last seven days and mark each one: "Only I can do this" or "Someone else could do this with clear instructions."

The second list is longer than you think. Start systematizing it. Start delegating it. Start building a company instead of owning a job.

The $1M mark isn't about revenue. It's about whether you built something that runs, or something that depends entirely on you showing up. Most painters choose the latter by default. You don't have to.
