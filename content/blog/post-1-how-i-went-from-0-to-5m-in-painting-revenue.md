# How I Went From $0 to $5M in Painting Revenue (And What I'd Do Differently)

I didn't start with capital. No investor. No inheritance. Just a truck I was still paying off, a Graco sprayer I bought used off Craigslist for $400, and the ability to outwork almost everyone around me.

That was year one. By year six, we were doing $5M a year in Buffalo, NY — a mid-size market with brutal winters and cheap competitors on every corner.

I'm not telling you this to impress you. I'm telling you because if I could do it here, in this market, with zero business background, you have no excuse. But you also need to hear the real version of this story — not the highlight reel. Because I made expensive mistakes, and some of them cost me years.

Here are the three turning points that actually moved the needle.

---

## Turning Point 1: Getting Off the Brush

I was a good painter. That was the problem.

For the first two years, I was on every job. Spraying, rolling, cutting in. Customers loved me, quality was high, and I was proud. I was also capping my business at whatever I could physically produce in a day. There's a ceiling on that number, and it's not pretty.

The turning point wasn't a lightbulb moment. It was exhaustion. I was running estimates in the morning, painting all day, doing invoices at night, and waking up to do it again. I had no time to sell, no time to hire, no time to think. I was an employee in my own company — just one who didn't get paid overtime.

I hired my first full-time painter when I honestly couldn't afford to. I figured out I needed to generate enough work to justify his wages before he even started. That pressure forced me to get serious about sales. I started running more estimates, following up consistently, and landing jobs I would have let slip before.

The math worked. I made less per job managing than I did painting — but I could manage five crews instead of standing on one ladder.

**If I did it again:** I'd get off the brush at month six, not year two. Every month you paint is a month you don't build.

---

## Turning Point 2: Building a Real Crew (Not Just Bodies)

Early on, I hired anyone who could hold a brush. That's a trap.

I had high turnover, inconsistent quality, and constant callbacks. Every bad hire cost me money twice — once in wages, once in fixing the work. I thought the solution was paying more. It wasn't.

The real fix was building an actual system: a short hiring process, a real onboarding checklist, clear quality standards, and a culture where good painters wanted to stay. When I started treating hiring like marketing — running it proactively, always keeping my pipeline warm, not hiring out of desperation — the crew got better. And when the crew got better, the referrals got better.

I went from three revolving-door guys to a core crew of eight people who'd been with me two or more years. That stability changed everything. My job costs got predictable. My quality went up. My stress went down.

**What I did wrong:** I hired fast when I was slammed and stopped hiring when things were slow. That's backwards. You should always be recruiting, even when you don't need anyone.

---

## Turning Point 3: Systematizing Sales

For years, I relied on referrals and my own personality to close jobs. That worked — until I wanted to grow beyond what I could personally touch.

The shift happened when I built a real sales process: a specific script for the estimate, a follow-up sequence that didn't rely on me remembering to call, and a way to track where every lead came from. Nothing fancy. A whiteboard, then a CRM.

Suddenly I could hand an estimate to someone else and know it would get closed at roughly the same rate. I could look at my numbers and know which marketing channels were actually working. I stopped running the business on gut feel and started running it on data.

Revenue went from $1.2M to $2.8M in two years. Not because I got smarter — because the system ran while I wasn't watching.

---

## What I'd Do Differently

**1. Raise prices sooner.** I left money on the table for years because I was scared of losing jobs. Turns out, raising your price 15% while closing 10% fewer jobs still makes you more money — and you work less doing it.

**2. Track every dollar from day one.** I flew blind for too long. Know your cost per lead, cost per acquisition, and job-level profit margins. If you don't know these numbers, you're not running a business. You're guessing.

**3. Hire for attitude, train for skill.** Some of my best painters had never held a brush before I hired them. Some of my worst were "experienced." Culture and work ethic are harder to teach than technique.

The $5M number didn't happen because I was special. It happened because I kept adjusting, kept building systems, and kept getting out of my own way. That's the job.

If you're sitting at $200K or $500K wondering how to break through, the answer is almost always the same: stop doing the thing that's capping you, and start building the thing that scales.

You already know how to paint. Now it's time to learn how to build.
